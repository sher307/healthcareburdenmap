About This Map

This interactive map visualizes three interconnected dimensions of health care inequality across the United States: the burden of medical debt by state, the concentration of private equity–owned hospitals, and the prevalence of health care deserts (defined as federally designated Health Professional Shortage Areas (HPSAs).

Together, these layers are intended to help users identify patterns in how financial hardship, ownership structures, and geographic access gaps may overlap across communities. To explore the map, toggle each layer on or off using the legend controls and click on any state to view a summary of available data for that region.

Data Sources & Limitations

Data for this map was drawn from several independent sources: Urban Institute medical debt trackage in the United States, the HRSA Data Warehouse for health care provider shortage area designations, and the PESP Private Equity Hospital Tracker for hospital ownership data.

While these sources are well-regarded, they each use different methodologies, time periods, and geographic units, so direct comparisons across layers should be made with care. The presence of private equity ownership, for example, does not on its own indicate poor outcomes — and a state with high medical debt may reflect insurance coverage gaps rather than care quality. This map is intended as an exploratory tool to surface questions worth investigating further, not as a basis for drawing firm conclusions about causation or policy outcomes.
